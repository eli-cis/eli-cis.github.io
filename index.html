01 , Introduction

THE ILLUSION OF OVERNIGHT SUCCESS

Music today is no longer limited to record stores or radio. It is released on digital platforms where millions of listeners can access it from anywhere in the world. Streaming services, social media, and independent distribution tools have completely changed the way artists share their work. And yet, with thousands of songs uploaded to platforms like Spotify and SoundCloud every single day, a serious question emerges: how can unknown or emerging artists compete in such a saturated industry?

I became interested in this question because of my own connection to music, not as a performer, but as someone who has watched people around me pour everything into their craft while remaining largely invisible to the wider world. I listen to a wide range of genres: metal, rock, emo, rap. Many of my favorite sounds come not from major labels but from underground scenes, collectives of artists making real, raw, meaningful work with no corporate backing and no guarantee of recognition. What I find most fascinating is the gap between what people see on social media, an artist seemingly blowing up overnight, and the years of grinding, experimenting, failing, and rebuilding that actually happened behind the scenes.

This essay enters an ongoing conversation about the role of digital tools and media in the modern music industry. On one side of the debate, scholars like Stephan Graham argue that digital technology has been genuinely transformative, that music is no longer "trapped" in the physical channels of market-controlled commodity exchange. On the other, researchers like David Hesmondhalgh and Leslie Meier remind us that these digital platforms are ultimately controlled by large technology companies whose algorithms determine what gets heard and what gets buried. My own position sits at the intersection of both views: while digital tools and media allow more artists than ever to create and share their work, success is not guaranteed by access alone. It depends on an artist's dedication, consistency, and strategic understanding of the platforms they use.

To explore this argument, I spoke with three people embedded in underground music scenes: Thomas Lemieux, guitarist for a punk band called Jaded in Paradise; B-Lo (Nigel), an independent hip-hop artist and member of the creative collective nobr8ks; and BK, the creative director behind the nobr8ks page. Their experiences, combined with the academic and cultural sources I have studied, paint a picture of an industry that is more accessible than ever, but no less competitive, and no less demanding of those who want to be heard.

Thesis: While digital tools and media allow more artists to create and share their work, success in the music industry is not guaranteed by access alone, it depends on an artist's dedication and strategic use of digital platforms.

02 , The Digital Shift

HOW DIGITAL TOOLS CHANGED EVERYTHING

To understand where underground artists stand today, it helps to understand how radically the music industry has changed in just a few decades. Not long ago, getting your music heard meant going through a major record label, signing contracts, handing over creative control, and relying on the label's marketing machinery to get your records into stores and onto radio stations. The artist was, in many ways, a product to be packaged and sold. The gatekeepers were few, and they were powerful.

That world still exists, but it no longer holds a monopoly. In Sounds of the Underground, musicologist Stephan Graham argues that the emergence of free digital tools of production, promotion, and dissemination has proved genuinely transformative. As he writes, "music is no longer trapped the way it once was in physical channels of market-controlled commodity exchange" (Graham 115). The shift Graham describes is visible in the most ordinary moments of daily life: the most prominently displayed items in a physical record store tend to be heavily marketed mainstream artists, artists backed by major label money. But online, the playing field looks different. An underground hip-hop producer from Connecticut can upload a track to Spotify on the same day as a major label release, and it will appear in the same search results.

David Hesmondhalgh and Leslie Meier, writing in Information, Communication & Society, offer a complementary but more cautious take. They document a series of "frequent, radical and disorienting shifts" in how music is accessed, a move from physical formats like CDs and vinyl to MP3 downloads, then to streaming, and now to the era of what they call "networked mobile personalisation," where music is something you access anywhere, anytime, through a connected device. This shift has dismantled old barriers to distribution but has raised new ones. The dominant forces shaping music consumption are no longer just record labels, they are technology companies: Spotify, Apple, YouTube, and Google. And these companies are not neutral platforms. Their recommendation algorithms, playlist curation systems, and advertising arrangements all influence which artists get discovered and which ones get lost.

What this means for underground artists is complicated, and it is worth pausing on a tension that Graham's optimism does not fully resolve. Graham argues that digital tools have freed music from physical, market-controlled channels, and that is true when it comes to distribution. But Thomas Lemieux, guitarist for Jaded in Paradise, pushed back on the idea that music creation itself has become more accessible. "There's a high cost of entry into music production," he told me, pointing to the expense of instruments, microphones, mixers, and amplifiers. For Thomas, the barrier is not getting music onto a platform, it is getting to the point where you have something worth uploading in the first place. Graham and Thomas are not necessarily disagreeing, but together they reveal an important distinction: digital tools have lowered the cost of distribution, while the cost of production remains a real obstacle for many aspiring artists. Accessibility, in other words, depends on where you are standing when you measure it.

Simply being on a platform does not guarantee that anyone will hear you. As I would come to understand through my interviews, the artists who find traction in this environment are not just talented, they are strategic.

The interviews revealed something important: underground music in Connecticut is not just a hobby for these artists, it is a mission. B-Lo described wanting to "make an impact" for where he is from, wanting to be the reason that people in Connecticut can wake up and chase a dream. BK described running the nobr8ks page not just as a social media manager, but as a strategist , someone who studies which colors and buzzwords work, which platform favors which video length, and how to build a visual identity that an algorithm will reward. And Thomas described the financial reality plainly: underground music does not pay much, if anything. Most money, he said, comes from merch sales. You do it because you love it.

04 , DIY Promotion

THE STRATEGY BEHIND THE POST

One of the most persistent myths about the digital music landscape is that going viral is a matter of luck, that the right song at the right moment will simply catch fire. My interviews pushed back hard against this idea. What I found instead was a picture of deeply intentional, labor-intensive self-promotion that requires not just creativity but fluency in the logic of platforms.

Robert Strachan, writing about micro-independent record labels in the UK, documents how independent artists have long relied on DIY networks, communities of musicians, fans, and distributors who support each other outside mainstream industry structures. He points to early examples of artists like the Arctic Monkeys, whose demos were posted as MP3s on internet discussion boards by fans, and Clap Your Hands Say Yeah, who sold albums directly from their website. These acts, Strachan argues, were "launched through concerted web-based campaigns to raise their profiles and build a fanbase" (Strachan). The tools have changed, Spotify and Instagram have replaced MP3 boards and band websites, but the underlying logic of DIY network-building remains the same.

BK's description of running the nobr8ks page echoes this logic precisely. When I asked him about the challenges of getting the collective noticed online, he said the hardest part was not the technology, it was the people. "If you don't have a good ecosystem and a team that everyone can step together on one accord," he explained, "you won't be able to even make it to the media platforms." Before any post goes up, the team has to agree on a direction: the script, the outfits, the visual style, the message. Only once that internal alignment is in place can the collective turn outward toward audiences and algorithms.

The algorithm will eat up quality work, not just rushing through things and trying to get something out for the sake of having it out.

, BK, Creative Director, nobr8ks

Thomas's experience with Jaded in Paradise tells a similar story from a different genre. The band uses Instagram as their primary promotional tool, posting photographs, artwork, show announcements, and merchandise. When fans come up to them after shows and ask when the next one is, the answer is always the same: follow us on Instagram. The platform functions as a central hub that bridges the live experience and the digital one, keeping fans connected between performances. Thomas described Instagram as "massively important" for the band's ability to get exposure, even though their shows remain their main source of new listeners.

What both BK and Thomas describe, without necessarily using the academic language, is what Strachan calls the DIY network: a web of mutual promotion, shared audiences, and collaborative visibility-building that operates outside of mainstream industry infrastructure. For nobr8ks, that network includes other underground artists, a shared Instagram aesthetic, and a deliberate approach to content strategy. For Jaded in Paradise, it includes the local show circuit, band photography, and the social currency of the live performance moment. Neither approach guarantees mainstream success. But both represent a coherent, strategic effort to be heard in a crowded space.

05 , Primary Source Analysis

"NINA": ACCESS WITHOUT VISIBILITY

"Nina," a collaborative track by underground artists Lush Soma and B-Lo, released independently on Spotify in 2026, illustrates the central tension of underground music in the digital age.

Listen , Spotify · "Nina" by Lushsoma & B-Lo

Lushsoma & B-Lo , "Nina." Independent release, 2026. As of March 30, 2026, the track had accumulated fewer than 100 streams, with B-Lo maintaining 34 monthly listeners on Spotify.

The song itself is a layered hip-hop track produced by Lush Soma using FL Studio, a digital audio workstation that has become the production tool of choice for countless independent producers. The instrumental incorporates modern beats and synthesized elements, structured around an introduction, verses, and a chorus, with B-Lo performing as the primary vocalist, delivering his verses with a rhythmic flow and auto-tune effects that place the song squarely within the contemporary underground hip-hop aesthetic.

What makes "Nina" significant for this essay is not what it sounds like, but what surrounds it. The track was promoted on both Lush Soma's and B-Lo's personal Instagram accounts and subsequently reposted by the official nobr8ks account, an example of exactly the kind of network-driven promotion that Strachan describes. Every repost is a small amplification: a few more eyes, a few more potential listeners. And yet, as of late March 2026, the track had fewer than 100 streams. B-Lo had 34 monthly listeners on Spotify.

Graham's argument that digital tools have made music globally accessible is true, "Nina" is on Spotify, available to anyone in the world with a streaming account. But Hesmondhalgh and Meier's warning about the power of technology companies is equally true: being on Spotify does not mean Spotify's algorithm will recommend you to anyone. The platform's recommendation systems favor artists who already have listeners. Discovery is, for most underground artists, not something the algorithm does for you. It is something you manufacture yourself, one post, one show, one repost at a time.

In my interview, B-Lo was candid about this reality. He described checking his numbers and feeling unmotivated, then dropping a new post and watching nearly a hundred likes come in within the first hours, despite having fewer than a thousand followers. He pulls in 40,000 views a month on Instagram. He is building something, slowly and deliberately, in a space that does not make it easy. His willingness to keep going, to treat the grind not as a detour but as the work itself, is, I think, what distinguishes underground artists who eventually break through from those who don't.

It's a grind, bro. It's going to suck, you have to go through the trial and error. That process is not fun. And a lot of people get turned off by it because they don't want to go through that.

, B-Lo (Nigel), nobr8ks
06 , Underground Networks

THE DOG POUND & THE DIY ECOSYSTEM

One of the most vivid illustrations of how underground communities use digital tools to sustain themselves is an Instagram post I came across from The Dog Pound, an underground music venue that uses social media to promote live shows featuring local artists.

Source , Instagram · @thedogp0und

The Dog Pound (@thedogp0und). "DION'S 20th BIRTHDAY 3/27 doors 6pm music 7pm 🐝 COME THRU!!" Instagram, 22 Mar. 2026. An event flyer promoting a live underground music show, featuring multiple artists and logistical details including parking information.

The post advertises an event called "Dion's 20th Birthday Bzzz," a show at The Dog Pound featuring a lineup of underground artists. The flyer itself is worth examining. It features abstract and experimental visuals: a bee, edited imagery, a woman in a dress, designed to catch the eye of a specific audience that already knows what The Dog Pound is and what kind of music it hosts.

The post also includes practical information: doors at 6pm, music at 7pm, parking details on the second slide. This combination of artistic expression and logistical communication is characteristic of how underground venues operate in digital spaces. They are not just broadcasting, they are talking to a community that is already invested. The Instagram post functions as both a promotional tool and a neighborhood bulletin board.

Tom mentioned that The Dog Pound is a venue where Jaded in Paradise would be playing soon, a detail that situates the post directly within the world of my interviews. Underground venues like The Dog Pound are a critical node in the DIY ecosystem Strachan describes. They provide performance spaces for artists who cannot get booked at mainstream venues, and they use social media to maintain a community of listeners who return show after show. The multi-artist lineup is also significant: by featuring multiple acts on the same bill, the venue effectively pools the audiences of every artist on the poster. Each artist's followers become potential fans of the others. This is community-based promotion , organic, collaborative, and free.

What the Dog Pound post makes clear is that digital tools do not replace the live experience for underground artists, they extend and amplify it. The show happens in physical space, but its reach extends across Instagram feeds, into the phones of people who might not have heard about it otherwise.

07 , Limitations & The Real Grind

ACCESS IS NOT ENOUGH

Everything I have explored in this essay points toward a single, uncomfortable truth: the democratization of music production and distribution has not democratized success. More people than ever can make music and share it with the world. But the music industry, even in its digital, decentralized form, remains deeply competitive, algorithmically shaped, and ultimately indifferent to effort alone.

Hesmondhalgh and Meier's analysis of how technology companies have taken over as the dominant force in music consumption is crucial here. Platforms like Spotify, YouTube, and Google are not neutral. Their algorithms are designed to maximize engagement and retention, which means they tend to recommend artists who already have listeners, content that already has momentum. For an artist just starting out, breaking into that recommendation loop is one of the central challenges of the modern music landscape. Thomas put it plainly from his own experience: "There's a high cost of entry into music production," he said, noting that instruments, microphones, mixers, and amplifiers represent a financial barrier that most aspiring artists have to clear before they can even think about algorithms and platforms. Hesmondhalgh and Meier show us that the system is stacked against the unknown artist once they are online; Thomas shows us that getting online in the first place is itself a privilege not everyone can afford. Together, they make the same point from two different angles: the playing field is not as level as it looks.

BK's insight about trends is perhaps the most philosophically interesting point raised in any of my interviews. He observed that artists who chase novelty, trying to create something entirely new, often end up recycling the past without realizing it. The current wave of rage and noise-influenced beats, the maximalist fashion aesthetics with colorful accessories, the obsession with going viral, all of it, he argued, is "just taking something that is recycled of old and giving it a slightly new modern twist." There is never really anything truly new; we take inspiration from our past and repackage it with our own feelings. This is not a cynical observation, it is, I think, a liberating one. It suggests that the pressure underground artists feel to be original or to go viral may be partly self-imposed. The real work is not to invent something new. It is to find your voice within a tradition and build a community around it.

Nobody can sound like me. I can't sound like Shoto, I can't sound like Lush. We're independent because we have our own style and our own creativity. And when we put that together, it's magic.

, B-Lo (Nigel), nobr8ks

This brings me back to my thesis. Digital tools and media have opened doors that were previously closed to artists without industry connections. The emergence of platforms like Spotify, Instagram, and YouTube has made it possible for a collective from Connecticut, a state that nobody talks about in the same breath as Atlanta or Los Angeles, to distribute music globally, build a visual identity, and cultivate a following from the ground up. That is real, and it matters. But those tools are not a shortcut. They are an infrastructure. What you build on that infrastructure, the quality, the consistency, the community, the strategy, is still entirely up to the artist.

For aspiring musicians and producers reading this: the grind is real. The numbers will be small at first, and there will be days when the gap between where you are and where you want to be feels insurmountable. But what B-Lo, BK, and Thomas all described, each in their own way, from their own corners of the underground, was a commitment to the process itself. Not to the outcome. The process: making the music, building the team, studying the platform, showing up to the show, posting again tomorrow. That, more than any algorithm, is what moves the needle.
